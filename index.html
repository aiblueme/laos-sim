Digital SIM

The eSIM Guide

eSIMs let you activate a data plan before your flight lands. Here's everything you need to know.

What is an eSIM?

An eSIM is a digital SIM card embedded in your phone. Instead of a plastic card, you scan a QR code to install a data plan — no swapping hardware, no queuing at an airport counter. Most flagship phones from 2020 onwards support eSIM.

Setting It Up

1

Check eSIM compatibility

Go to Settings → General → About on iPhone, or Settings → Network on Android. If you see "eSIM" or "Digital SIM" you're good to go.

2

Purchase and download before you travel

Set up your eSIM profile on Wi-Fi at home. This is especially important for Internet in Laos and Gigago — both require online registration at the time of purchase.

3

Install the eSIM profile

Scan the QR code sent by your provider, or use the provider's app. Confirm the profile appears in your phone's cellular settings.

4

Activate on first use

Most plans activate automatically when you first connect to a Laos tower. Set your eSIM as the preferred data line in your phone settings before landing.

5

Keep your home SIM for emergencies

Dual SIM means you can still receive calls and texts on your home number while using the eSIM for data. Turn off data roaming on your home SIM to avoid surprise charges.

6

Passport registration (since October 2024)

All eSIMs for Laos require passport registration. For international providers this is done online at checkout; for Internet in Laos / Gigago it happens during purchase. This is standard across Southeast Asia.

On the Ground

How to Buy a SIM

Practical steps for picking up a local SIM once you land — or before you leave home.

At the Airport

  1. Unitel and LTC counters are at Wattay Airport (Vientiane) and Luang Prabang Airport.
  2. Have your passport ready — registration is mandatory since October 2024 and takes about 5 minutes.
  3. Airport prices can be 20–30% higher than city shops, but plans are still cheap by global standards.
  4. City shops offer more plan variety if you can wait — especially the Unitel flagship stores in Vientiane.

In the City

  • Unitel shops, convenience stores, and markets stock SIMs throughout Laos.
  • In Vientiane, 7-Eleven locations carry SIMs and top-up vouchers.
  • Staff at dedicated operator shops speak enough English for the transaction.
  • Look for the official Unitel blue-and-white or LTC branding — avoid touts offering SIMs on the street.

ID Requirements

  • Your passport is required for all SIM and eSIM purchases under October 2024 regulations.
  • Physical SIMs: shop staff scan and register on the spot.
  • International eSIMs: passport details submitted during online checkout.
  • Internet in Laos / Gigago eSIMs: registration completed during online purchase.
  • This is standard across Southeast Asia and is not unusual.

Topping Up

  • Top-up vouchers available at convenience stores, markets, and 7-Eleven (Vientiane).
  • Dial *121# to check your Unitel balance.
  • Use the Unitel app for in-app top-up (easier than scratch cards).
  • Only use official retailers or the app — fake scratch cards exist.

Watch out for these

  • Airalo Laos eSIM → ETL network: Well-documented issue — Airalo's Laos-country plan uses the weak ETL network. Always choose the Asialink regional plan instead.
  • "Tourist packages" from strangers: Buy SIMs only from official operator shops or recognised retailers, not from touts at bus stations or tourist areas.
  • Unofficial top-up sellers: Stick to convenience stores, official app, or operator shops. Fake scratch cards do circulate.
  • Ride-hailing apps need a local number: Loca and LCR require a Lao phone number to register. International eSIMs (Airalo, Holafly, Nomad) provide data only — no number. Get a local number from Unitel, Internet in Laos, or Gigago.
Questions & Answers

Common Questions

The things travellers ask most before and after landing in Laos.

Which SIM gives the best coverage in rural Laos?
Unitel, without question. It holds roughly 50% market share and has won the Ookla Speedtest Award for fastest mobile network in Laos. It's the only reliable option in places like the Plain of Jars, Nong Khiaw, and remote northern trekking routes. LTC (Lao Telecom) is a decent backup in the south.
Is the Airalo Laos eSIM any good?
The Laos-country plan is best avoided. It runs on the ETL network, which is weak outside Vientiane. Multiple travel bloggers and forums explicitly warn against it. If you want to use Airalo, choose the Asialink regional plan instead — it connects to stronger networks including Unitel.
Do I need my passport to buy a SIM in Laos?
Yes, a passport is required for all SIM and eSIM purchases since October 2024. For physical SIMs, shop staff scan it on the spot (takes about 5 minutes). For eSIMs, you submit passport details during online checkout. This is standard across Southeast Asia and is nothing to worry about.
Can I get a SIM at the airport?
Yes — Wattay International Airport (Vientiane) and Luang Prabang Airport both have Unitel and/or LTC counters in arrivals. Prices at the airport are typically 20–30% higher than city shops. Still cheap in absolute terms, and often the most convenient option if you land late.
What internet speeds can I expect?
On Unitel 4G in Vientiane and Luang Prabang, expect 20–60 Mbps download — plenty for video calls and streaming. In smaller towns, speeds drop to 5–20 Mbps. Remote and rural areas get 1–5 Mbps on 3G, and occasionally no signal at all. Vientiane has 5G coverage since 2025, which is noticeably faster.
Can I use a Laos SIM to call an Uber / ride-hailing app?
Uber doesn't operate in Laos. The local ride-hailing apps are Loca and LCR — both require a local Lao phone number to register. International eSIMs (Airalo, Holafly, Nomad, etc.) are data only and won't give you a Lao number. To get one, buy a Unitel physical SIM, or use Internet in Laos or Gigago eSIM — both include a local number.
Is Holafly worth the price for Laos?
Holafly is around 7–10× more expensive than a local Unitel SIM for similar or less data. It runs on the Unitel network, so coverage is solid. It's worth considering if you genuinely use a lot of data, hate the idea of running out, or want to avoid any paperwork at all. For most budget and mid-range travellers, a local SIM or Internet in Laos eSIM will serve better.
What's the cheapest way to get data before I land?
Internet in Laos (shop.internetlaos.com) or Gigago — both offer eSIMs on the Unitel network at near-local prices (from ~$5). You can buy, install, and activate before your flight. Once you're in Laos, switching to a physical Unitel SIM will always be cheaper, but these two eSIMs are the best pre-arrival option.
Do I need to top up my SIM in Laos?
If you buy a plan with a set validity (e.g. 30 days, 30 GB), you generally don't need to top up unless you run out of data or the plan expires. To check your Unitel balance, dial *121#. Top-up vouchers are available at convenience stores and 7-Eleven, or use the Unitel app. Stick to official retailers — fake scratch cards do exist.
Will my eSIM work in Thailand, Vietnam, or Cambodia too?
If you buy a regional Asia plan (like Airalo Asialink, Nomad Asia, or Holafly's multi-country options), yes — it'll cover multiple countries. A Laos-specific local SIM or eSIM generally won't roam to neighbouring countries, or will charge high roaming fees. If you're island-hopping across several countries, a regional eSIM makes more sense despite the higher cost per GB.
